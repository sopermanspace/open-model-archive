Monorepos Are Overprescribed — And Your Team Size Is the Real Variable

The tech industry has a persistent habit of treating the architectural choices of trillion-dollar behemoths as universal truths. Somewhere along the line, the monorepo became the default prescription for every engineering organization experiencing growing pains. The pitch is undeniably seductive: atomic commits, a single source of truth, and the absolute elimination of dependency drift. But for the vast majority of engineering teams, adopting a monorepo is an act of premature optimization that actively destroys velocity. The harsh reality is that the monorepo is not a silver bullet; it is an organizational scaling mechanism, and its true value is entirely dependent on the size of your team.

The Tooling Tax and Google Envy

When you adopt a monorepo, you are not just changing where your code lives—you are fundamentally altering your relationship with infrastructure. Small to mid-sized teams frequently underestimate the sheer gravitational pull of maintaining a unified repository. They suffer from "Google Envy," adopting patterns built for tens of thousands of engineers without possessing the dedicated developer productivity teams required to sustain them.

Consider the all-too-common trajectory of a mid-stage startup. I once observed an engineering organization of twenty developers at a fintech company, let's call them LedgerFlow, who decided their scattered microservices were too difficult to test end-to-end. Their solution was to migrate everything into a single repository powered by Bazel. The theoretical goal was seamless shared libraries and atomic refactoring. The practical result was that their most senior product engineers spent the next four months fighting cache invalidation issues, debugging esoteric CI pipeline timeouts, and writing custom tooling just to get their local builds under ten minutes. They solved their dependency problem by introducing an infrastructure tax they simply could not afford to pay.

The Validity of Atomic Commits

To be fair, the proponents of monorepos are not wrong about the pain of polyrepos. Anyone who has manually bumped a core authentication library version across thirty different microservices knows the acute misery of dependency hell. Diamond dependency conflicts and asynchronous deployments can quickly turn a microservice architecture into a distributed monolith that is impossible to safely update. In these scenarios, the monorepo’s promise of atomic commits—where a change to a core library and the updating of all its consumers happen in a single operation—is genuinely transformative. If you have hundreds of engineers working across deeply intertwined domains, the coordination cost of separate repositories eventually exceeds the infrastructure cost of a monorepo.

Aligning Architecture with Reality

But that crossover point happens much later than most engineering leaders want to admit. If your engineering department fits comfortably on a single floor, or if you can still recognize everyone on your Zoom all-hands, a monorepo is likely going to slow you down. At that scale, the friction of maintaining multiple repositories is a communication problem, not an architectural one. You don't need a unified, bespoke build graph; you need better internal API contracts and a little organizational discipline.

We need to stop prescribing enterprise-scale solutions to mid-market problems. Architecture should reflect the reality of your organizational chart, not the aspirational engineering blogs of a FAANG company. If you are spending more time configuring your build system than writing business logic, you haven't solved your scaling problem—you've just successfully distracted yourself from it.

Monorepos Are Overprescribed — And Your Team Size Is the Real Variable

The engineering landscape is currently experiencing a peculiar blend of utopian dogma and technical overreach. Nearly every major tech blog, conference keynote, and venture capital deck whispers—at times, shouts—about the salvation found within the monolithic repository: the monorepo. We are sold a vision of perfect dependency graph clarity, instant cross-team discoverability, and ultimate architectural purity.

But let's be honest about what this obsession with tooling often distracts us from. The relentless pursuit of the "perfect" repo structure—the belief that hoisting everything into one single source of truth is inherently superior—is not an architectural solution; it is an organizational coping mechanism for process debt. Monorepos are treated like magic bullets, when in reality, they are merely sophisticated tooling choices that do not solve underlying problems with human coordination, communication latency, or team maturity.

The biggest flaw in the monorepo discourse is its failure to distinguish between scaling architectural complexity and simply managing the sheer size of a growing organization. These monolithic structures promise seamless collaboration by default, but often end up replacing local coupling with systemic, build-time dependencies that are far harder to manage. The engineering pain point rarely resides in where the code lives; it resides in the coordination required when multiple teams believe they have full ownership and autonomy over interdependent components.

Consider a team like "Phoenix," tasked with managing an increasingly complex payments gateway. They migrated from isolated repositories (polyrepo) into a monorepo hoping for better visibility. Initially, the build speed was fast and dependency resolution seemed magical. However, within six months, feature development ground to a halt not because of technical limits, but because the sheer scope of change required an unprecedented level of coordination across five distinct teams. Making a seemingly minor API change in the payments-core directory required mandatory review, testing, and scheduled release gates involving every single component that touched payments—a bureaucracy that was technically enforced by the repo structure itself.

This anecdote reveals the true variable: it is not the repository model; it is the team’s ability to establish meaningful boundaries. A healthy design does not emerge from a monolithic blast radius; it emerges from clear, agreed-upon contracts between services, regardless of whether those services reside in separate Git repositories or adjacent directories.

The counterargument often pitched against this critique suggests that polyrepos inherently lead to version drift and dependency hell, making the monorepo an operational necessity. While managing dependency graphs across service boundaries is a legitimate challenge, confusing structural convenience with organizational efficacy misunderstands systems engineering at its core. Strong governance—robust CI/CD pipelines, comprehensive API contract testing, and mandatory RFC processes—are the true boundary enforcers, not filesystem layout.

If your primary motivation for adopting a monorepo isn't solving a verifiable technical impediment like deep dependency ambiguity or cross-repo atomic commits, then you are simply chasing a fashionable abstraction. You risk exchanging architectural simplicity for structural rigidity. Remember that great software doesn't scale because it lives in one place; it scales because its teams know exactly who to talk to, and when.

Monorepos Are Overprescribed — And Your Team Size Is the Real Variable

The monorepo has become the architectural equivalent of a standing desk: recommended by everyone, purchased in bulk, and quietly hated by the people who actually have to use it. For the last decade, engineering blogs have sold the single repository as a cure-all for consistency, cross-team collaboration, and "the way the best companies work." But the real variable that determines whether a monorepo will accelerate or suffocate your engineering culture is not your deployment topology or your language stack. It is team size — and most teams adopting monorepos today are nowhere near large enough to justify the tax.

The Convenience Trap

At small scale, monorepos feel magical. You rename a shared library, update every caller, and ship one atomic commit. No versioning dance, no cross-repo PRs, no "who owns this package?" existential crisis. I watched Team Sprocket at Rivet hit exactly this honeymoon phase. With eight engineers, their single repository was a shared campfire: everyone knew the songs, and if someone moved a log, the group noticed immediately. Then the team grew to thirty-five. The same repository became a parking garage where every car was connected to every other car by a tow cable. A minor dependency bump triggered four-hour CI queues. Refactors that once took an afternoon now required a migration calendar. The tool they had adopted for velocity had become the reason velocity stalled.

The Team-Size Pivot Point

This is the pivot point nobody talks about. Monorepos start paying dividends when coordination costs between repositories exceed the friction of a single shared space. That crossover usually happens at an engineering population far larger than the average Series B or mid-market company. Below that threshold, the costs are real and the benefits are theoretical: you are not doing Google-scale refactors; you are fighting Bazel, Nx, or a homegrown build cache so you can merge a one-line change in a service you do not own. The larger the population, the more a monorepo's atomicity and visibility justify the tooling investment. The smaller the population, the more it resembles centralized planning for a village.

What Monorepos Still Get Right

None of this means monorepos are wrong universally. When you have hundreds of engineers shipping interdependent products, a single source of truth for dependencies, APIs, and shared infrastructure is a genuine force multiplier. Atomic cross-service refactors and universal dependency upgrades can prevent the version-matrix hell that multi-repo companies accept as fate. The mistake is not that monorepos exist; it is that they have been promoted from a scaling strategy to a default morality. A team of twelve copying the repository strategy of a company with twelve thousand engineers is not learning from the best — it is cosplaying as them.

The next time someone proposes a monorepo, do not ask which CI tool they prefer. Ask how many engineers will actually live inside it. If the answer does not justify the empire, stop building the palace.
