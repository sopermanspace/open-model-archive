Explain Git Rebase as Cooking

Accurate git concepts via kitchen metaphors — different comedic frame than the golden retriever task.

Explain **`git rebase`** to a home cook who has never used version control.

## Rules

1. Stay technically accurate — commits, branch, replay, conflicts must be correct
2. Use cooking/kitchen metaphors throughout (prep bowls, sauce reduction, mise en place)
3. Contrast rebase with merge using one food analogy
4. Length: 250–400 words
5. The cook should "respond" once at the end with a single sentence in quotes

Output plain text only. No title, no markdown headers.

Imagine you are developing a master recipe for a stew. Every time you finish a step, like chopping onions or searing meat, you write it down. Those recorded steps are your "commits."

You decide to experiment with a spicy flavor profile, so you create a "branch." This is like setting up a separate prep station. Over here, you make a few new commits: you toast chilies, grind them, and stir them in. 

While you are doing this, your sous chef updates the master stew recipe on the main counter by adding a crucial step: deglazing the pot with wine. 

Now your spicy variation is out of date. You want that wine flavor in your spicy stew. You have two choices: merge or rebase.

A "merge" is like dumping your spicy prep bowl directly into the main pot with the wine. The flavors combine, but your recipe journal gets messy. It suddenly reads like two people shouting instructions at once, mixing up who did what and when. 

A "rebase" is a much cleaner approach to your mise en place. When you rebase, you temporarily set your spicy commits aside. You update your prep station to match the exact current state of the main recipe, including that new wine reduction. Then, you "replay" your spicy commits one by one on top of this fresh base. First, you toast the chilies, then grind them, then stir them in. 

Your recipe journal remains a single, perfectly linear sequence of instructions, as if you had planned to add the wine before the chilies all along. 

Sometimes, while replaying your steps, you hit a "conflict." Maybe the master recipe now says to puree the stew, but your spicy step says to leave it chunky. You have to pause, taste, and manually resolve whether you want a smooth or chunky spicy stew before you can finish rewriting the steps.

"Ah, so rebasing is just rewriting my recipe steps as if I had the newest ingredients from the start, rather than just throwing everything into a pot together!"

Imagine your main project is a huge family recipe book, filled with notes about different ingredients and techniques—these are your commits. When you work on a feature (like making a new side dish), you create a private set of notes called a branch.

Sometimes, while you're perfecting your sauce reduction, your partner has also been working on something else entirely (maybe updating the pantry inventory) on the main recipe book. You need to incorporate their changes into your work. That’s where `git merge` comes in. It takes both sets of notes and combines them, creating a special "merge commit" that marks exactly when the two paths joined up.

`git rebase` is much more delicate. Instead of adding a merge note, it acts like carefully peeling your current set of notes (your feature) off the main book, resetting your private branch right to the latest point where the main book and your work separated, and then meticulously reapplying every single one of your original commits, one by one, on top of the new foundation. It's not just combining; it's rewriting history so that it looks like you started your work *after* all their changes were already in place.

Think of `git merge` as putting two completed dishes side-by-side at a feast—you see both paths of flavor development, and the join point is visible. `git rebase`, however, is like perfecting one complex dessert: you take your recipe steps and rewrite them so they naturally flow from the ingredients that were just added to the main kitchen counter, making it look as if all changes happened sequentially on a single timeline. Because you are rewriting history, every ingredient (commit) gets renamed and placed in a new location.

"It sounds like using rebase is cleaner because I don't want my recipe book to have too many historical notes about when things were combined."

Imagine your kitchen has the master recipe on the counter—call it main. You grab a prep bowl to experiment in private—say, a branch named garlic-butter-pasta. Every change you make is a commit: a single recorded step like mincing garlic, melting butter, or tossing the noodles.

While you are working, someone else updates the master recipe on the counter—perhaps they changed the sauce base. Your prep bowl still started from the older version. Rebase is like lifting your prep bowl off the counter, moving its starting point to the freshest master recipe, and then replaying your steps one by one on top: mince garlic, melt butter, toss noodles. Git takes each of your commits and reapplies it in order to the new tip.

If one of your steps now clashes with the updated master recipe—say the main sauce now simmers for ten minutes but you reduced yours to five—Git pauses the rebase. That is a conflict. You taste, adjust the dish, and then tell Git to continue so the remaining steps can be replayed.

The result is a clean, linear history. Your garlic-butter-pasta branch looks as if you had been cooking from the latest master recipe all along, because rebase rewrites the history of your branch rather than weaving two histories together.

Merge works differently. Merge would combine both versions into one finished dish and leave both histories visible, like serving two sauces side by side on the plate. Rebase is more like reducing those sauces into one smooth glaze.

One warning: do not rebase commits you have already shared with other cooks, because you are rewriting recipe cards they may have already copied into their own kitchens.

"So rebase is basically wiping my station, starting from the freshest recipe, and redoing my steps so the whole dish looks like I planned it that way from the start."
